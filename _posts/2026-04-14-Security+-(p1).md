**Security Control Categories:**

Security controls is a topic that covers the different aspect of controlling risks and hazards of cyber security.

These are used to prevent a security event from occurring, minimize the impact of an event or limit/mitigate the damage that occurs if said event takes place.

There are different security controls that are split into a few broad categories.

- **Technical controls**
- **Managerial controls**
- **Operational controls**
- **Physical controls**
  
**Technical Controls** — These are controls that are set up within the system that you are protecting, such as firewalls, antivirus or other bespoke methods. These are software based.

**Managerial Controls** - These controls are administrative, “paper” based such as legislation, operating procedures, training courses, awareness programs.

**Operational Control**s — These are controls that are implemented by "People” not systems, these include Security guards, Calling the authorities in the event on an incident, reporting issues, Patrolling, incident hand over briefing etc.

**Physical Controls** — These controls are in the physical world and block/deter physical access to areas, such as locks, barriers, key cards, alarms etc

**Further control descriptors:**

There are also **Deterrent, detective, Corrective, Compensating & Directive**. Additionally remember that many cover multiple areas (e.g. CCTV could be a Deterrent & detective)
on

**Deterrent** — the control may not physically or logically prevent access, but psychologically discourages an attacker from attempting an intrusion, This could include signs and warnings of legal penalties against trespass or intrusion.

**Detective** — Allows you to be able to garner information on security events, CCTV, digital logs, log in screens, intruder alarms. It may/may not prevent access to the site/system.

**Corrective** — Once an event has taken place what can be done to mitigate the issue, think Fire extinguishers, emergency procedures, backup /restore data.

**Compensating** — these are additional resources/means in which you can control & mitigate security events. Think about having in a backup generator if the power cuts out. Their use could be temporary. This could also be that you disable some functionality of application if there are security concerns whilst you wait to get it patched. It could also mean hiring additional staff to prevent exploitation of system weaknesses.

**Directive** — This is where a “subject” is directed towards security compliance, “Do This” etc. This seems very similar to both Managerial/Operative controls., After some research Directive Control is more specifically explicit and definitive. Rather than Managerial is more broad and includes multiple facets rather than just dealing with directing people.



**The CIA Triad:** Foundation of Cybersecurity
At the core of cybersecurity are three main goals, often referred to as the CIA Triad:

- **Confidentiality**: Information should only be accessible to people who are authorized to see it.
- **Integrity**: Data should not be changed without permission. It should remain accurate and trustworthy.
- **Availability**: Authorized users should be able to access information when needed.

Together, these three form the backbone of any secure system. To support these goals, we apply access control mechanisms.



Non-Repudiation — Ensuring the sender of data cannot deny having sent it.

Non-Repudiation Use Cases:
- e-Government systems: To prevent users from denying their submissions.
- Online banking: So that users can’t deny financial transactions.
- Digital signatures: To make signed documents legally binding.
- Secure emails: To confirm the actual sender of the message.

Only asymmetric encryption provides true non-repudiation, using private key signatures.

Don’t Build Your Own Algorithms

Always use publicly tested, industry-standard algorithms (e.g., AES, RSA). Avoid creating custom encryption methods.



Non-repudiation is a security measure that guarantees a message or action can be incontrovertibly attributed to its sender. By providing undeniable evidence of origin, it prevents any party from later denying their participation. This concept is essential in environments where accountability and trust are critical, such as financial transactions, healthcare data management, and e-commerce operations.
For instance, if an individual sends an email or initiates a transaction, non-repudiation ensures they cannot later claim non-involvement. Techniques such as digital signatures, Public Key Infrastructure (PKI), audit logs, and timestamping establish a verifiable record of a message’s origin and its integrity.
